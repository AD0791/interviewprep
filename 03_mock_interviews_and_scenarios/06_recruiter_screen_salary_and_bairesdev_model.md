# The Recruiter Screen: Salary, the BairesDev Model, and What Happens Next

Everything else in this repo prepares you to impress an engineer. This file prepares you for the other half of Wednesday's call: the parts where the recruiter is not testing your knowledge but filtering on business criteria. Candidates fail screens on these questions more often than on technical ones, usually because they treat them as small talk and improvise. Do not improvise. The recruiter has a checklist; here is what is on it and what to say.

## How BairesDev Actually Works (and why it changes the questions)

BairesDev is not a product company hiring you to build its own software. It is a technology services and staff-augmentation firm: US and international clients come to BairesDev needing engineers, and BairesDev places its own employees onto those client teams, working remotely and aligned to US time zones. You would be employed (typically as a long-term contractor) by BairesDev but doing your daily work embedded in a client's team — their standups, their codebase, their product.

This model explains every question in the screen. English fluency matters because you will speak to the client daily, not just to BairesDev. The salary question comes early and bluntly because your rate must fit under what clients pay for your seniority band — it is a hard filter, not an opening move in a negotiation. The CV-coherence check matters because BairesDev's product *is* your profile: they will present it to clients, and it has to survive a client's scrutiny. And the "brief theory questions" exist because the recruiter needs enough signal to route you to the right technical assessment track.

Your profile fits this model unusually well, and you should say so when the recruiter explains the company: you have already spent five-plus years as exactly this kind of engineer — a remote contractor embedded with US and international organizations, in English, across time zones. Working the BairesDev way is not a change for you; it is your track record.

## The Salary Question

Expect it in the first ten minutes, phrased as "What are your salary expectations?" — usually in USD per month, since BairesDev contracts most LATAM-region engineers monthly. Three rules govern your answer.

First, give a range, not a number, and give it without hesitation. Hesitation reads as either inexperience or an invitation to anchor you low. Say it plainly:

> "I'm looking for something in the range of [your range] USD per month. I have some flexibility for the right long-term engagement, but that's the range I'm targeting for a senior fullstack Python role."

Second, if the recruiter presses for a single number, name the upper-middle of your range and attach it to the value: "If I had to name one number, [figure] — that reflects five years of remote delivery for US clients across the full stack, backend through data through frontend."

Third, know before the call what your walk-away floor is, and do not say yes on the call to anything below it. It is always acceptable to respond to a low band with: "That's below my target range, but I'd like to continue the process and see the full picture of the role first." The screen is a filter, not a final negotiation — your only goals are to avoid being filtered out for aiming absurdly high and to avoid trapping yourself with a low anchor you resent later.

Fill in your range before Tuesday evening and rehearse the sentence with real numbers, out loud, until it sounds boring. A salary sentence should sound like a fact, not a question.

## The Process Map (so "next steps" holds no surprises)

BairesDev's pipeline is longer than most companies': after this screen come automated online tests — typically a timed cognitive/logic test (CCAT-style) and sometimes technical multiple-choice — then one or more technical interviews with engineers, and finally matching with a specific client, which can involve a client interview. The recruiter will walk you through this at the end of the call. Your job is simply to not be surprised, to ask when the tests arrive ("Will I receive the assessment link today, and is there a deadline?"), and to sound ready rather than daunted. The [CCAT primer](08_ccat_and_online_tests_primer.md) covers the test itself.

## Your Questions for the Final Five Minutes

Asking nothing signals low interest; asking about vacation signals wrong priorities. Ask two or three of these, which signal that you already understand the model:

> "When you match engineers to clients, how much weight do you give to domain background? I'm asking because mine is data-heavy — dashboards, pipelines, analytics — and I'd love to land with a client where that compounds."

> "What does the timeline usually look like between passing the technical interviews and starting with a client?"

> "For fullstack Python profiles like mine, what are clients asking for most right now — is it more backend-weighted or evenly split with React?"

## Interview Angles

**"What are your salary expectations?"** — Deliver the rehearsed sentence with your real range: "I'm looking for something in the range of [your range] USD per month. I have some flexibility for the right long-term engagement, but that's the range I'm targeting for a senior fullstack Python role." Then stop talking. Silence after a salary answer is negotiating strength; filling it is how people talk themselves down.

**"Are you comfortable working with US time zones and communicating in English every day?"** — "Yes — that has been my daily reality for over five years. My clients at Tekkod, HANWASH, and Caris Foundation were all US-based or international, all remote, and English has been my working language throughout. I'm in the Eastern-Caribbean time band, so US business hours are my normal hours."

**"Why are you looking to leave your current position?"** — Never badmouth Tekkod; frame it as seeking scale and stability. "I'm not running from anything — Tekkod has been a great engagement and my current modernization project is nearly delivered. I'm looking for what BairesDev specifically offers: long-term placement with a larger client team, more senior engineers around me, and steady fullstack Python work where the data side of my background gets used every day."
